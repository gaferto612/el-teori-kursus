## 7.1 Structure of the atom

An **atom** consists of:

| Particle | Symbol | Charge | Location |
|----------|--------|--------|----------|
| Proton | p⁺ | +1 | Atomic nucleus |
| Neutron | n⁰ | 0 (neutral) | Atomic nucleus |
| Electron | e⁻ | −1 | Shells around the nucleus |

> 💡 The nucleus contains almost all of the atom's mass. Electrons orbit the nucleus in specific energy shells.

### Key numbers
- Number of protons = the **atomic number** (Z) — determines which element it is
- Number of protons = number of electrons in a neutral atom
- The nucleus diameter is ~10⁻¹⁵ m, the atom diameter is ~10⁻¹⁰ m (10,000 times larger)

---

## 7.2 Electron shells

Electrons are arranged in **shells** (K, L, M, N ...) with capacity:

| Shell | Max electrons |
|-------|---------------|
| K (n=1) | 2 |
| L (n=2) | 8 |
| M (n=3) | 18 |
| N (n=4) | 32 |

**Valence electrons** = the electrons in the **outermost** shell. They determine the chemical and electrical properties of the atom.

---

## 7.3 The periodic table

Mendeleev's periodic table organises all 100+ elements by atomic number.

| Group | Property | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 1 (alkali metals) | 1 valence electron — very reactive | Li, Na, K |
| 2 (alkaline earth) | 2 valence electrons | Mg, Ca |
| 11 | Excellent conductors | Cu, Ag, Au |
| 14 | **Semiconductors** | C, Si, Ge |
| 17 (halogens) | 7 valence electrons — very reactive | F, Cl, Br |
| 18 (noble gases) | 8 valence electrons — full — inert | He, Ne, Ar |

> ⚡ **Key electrical insight**: Materials with **few valence electrons** (1–3) are good **conductors**. Materials with **many valence electrons** (5–7) are **insulators**. Materials with **4 valence electrons** (Si, Ge) are **semiconductors** — the foundation of modern electronics.

## 7.4 Conductors, insulators and semiconductors

### Conductors
Atoms with **few** valence electrons (typically 1) — these "free" electrons can move easily through the material.

**Examples**: Silver (best), copper, gold, aluminium.

| Material | Resistivity ρ (Ω·mm²/m) |
|----------|-------------------------|
| Silver | 0.016 |
| Copper | 0.0175 |
| Aluminium | 0.028 |
| Iron | 0.1 |

### Insulators
Many valence electrons — strongly bound in the molecular structure.

**Examples**: Glass, rubber, plastic (PVC, PE), porcelain, dry air, mineral oil.

### Semiconductors
4 valence electrons — conductivity can be modified by **doping**.

**Examples**: Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge).

## 7.5 Ions and chemical bonds

### Ion
An atom that has lost or gained electrons and therefore has an electric charge.

- **Cation** = positive ion (lost electron) — e.g. Na⁺, Cu²⁺
- **Anion** = negative ion (gained electron) — e.g. Cl⁻, O²⁻

### Bond types

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Ionic bond | Between metal and non-metal | NaCl (table salt) |
| Covalent bond | Atoms share electrons | H₂O, CO₂ |
| Metallic bond | Free electrons in "electron gas" | Cu, Fe, Al |

## 7.6 Chemistry in practice — electrically relevant applications

### Galvanic cell (battery)

Two different metals in an electrolyte produce a voltage via chemical reaction.

**Example — Daniell cell**:
- Zinc (Zn) in ZnSO₄
- Copper (Cu) in CuSO₄
- Voltage: about 1.1 V

**Common battery types**:
| Type | Voltage | Use |
|------|---------|-----|
| Alkaline | 1.5 V | Household |
| Lead-acid | 2 V/cell (12 V total) | Car battery |
| Li-ion | 3.7 V/cell | Mobile, laptop |
| NiMH | 1.2 V | Rechargeable |

### Corrosion
Unwanted chemical breakdown of metal — e.g. iron → rust (Fe₂O₃).

**Protection**:
- **Galvanisation** (zinc coating) — zinc "sacrifices" itself
- **Paint/varnish**
- **Cathodic protection** (sacrificial anode)

### Water treatment in boilers
> 🔥 **Boiler-operator note**: Hard water contains Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions which can deposit limescale in the boiler. Feed water is therefore softened — typically with an ion exchanger (Na cycle).

---

## 7.7 pH value

pH = measure of acidity of a solution.

| pH | Type | Example |
|----|------|---------|
| 0–6 | Acidic | Lemon juice (2), vinegar (2.5) |
| 7 | Neutral | Pure water |
| 8–14 | Basic | Soap (10), sodium hydroxide (14) |

> 💧 Boiler water is typically kept slightly basic (pH 9–11) to minimise corrosion.

---

## 📌 Summary

- **The atom** consists of protons (+) and neutrons (neutral) in the nucleus, with electrons (−) in orbitals.
- **The number of valence electrons** determines whether a material is a conductor, insulator or semiconductor.
- **Conductors** (e.g. copper) have 1 valence electron, **insulators** have 7–8, **semiconductors** (silicon) have 4.
- **The galvanic cell** (battery): two different metals in an electrolyte produce a voltage.
- **Corrosion**: chemical reaction that destroys metal — countered by paint, galvanisation, or cathodic protection.
- **In boilers**: hard water causes lime deposits — water is softened before entering the boiler.
